The atharvan-s describe two sets of core holy/medicinal/magical plants. One set is said to be the oShadhi-s of the atharvan-s or bhR^igu-s:

chiti, prAyashchiti, shami,  shamikA, shavaMsha, shAmyavAka, talAsha, palAsha, vAshA, shiMshapa, shimbala, sipuNA or karI, darbha  and apAmArga. 

This set is pacificatory and soothing.

The other set is the a~Ngirasa oShadhi-s, which are: kapu, viparvA, rodAkA, vR^ikkAvatI, nADA and nirdahantI. 

This set is attacking.

The identities of many of these remain obscure to the modern AV practitioner, and he ends up using substitutes or just a limited set for nominal adherence to scripture. 

Nevertheless, a subset of these are likely of Harappan vintage and surviving loans from the now extinct Harappan language into Indo-Aryan. However, it is clear that on settling in India, the atharvan-s did not simply adopt Harappan names for all the plants they adopted. Some of these might have been by sonic similarity, but some were derived based on the features of the plant. For example, vR^ikkAvati could have been based on kidney like beans or leaves of the plant. 

If you are not prone to autochthonism then you could engage in the exercise of classifying these names in these categories.
