## dhI
Source: [TW](https://x.com/blog_supplement/status/2053719048597188864)

One of the striking differences between the hautra and the Adhvaryava tradition is the use of the root "dhI-". It seems to be at least 7 times more commonly used in the RV than in a yajurvedic text, like the taittirIya saMhitA. 

We believe this marks a true mechanistic difference between the two old vaidika stream, with one focused on capturing the "dhI" in the form of sUkta-s and the other on incantations accompanying detailed ritual actions. The English translations of dhI mostly render it as "insight", "vision", or "inspiration". 

To be more precise, it seems to capture the insight of a R^iShi, which leads him to "seeing" the mantra – thus he becomes its seer. Hence, it represents the very abstraction of the mantra itself that is first seen by a R^iShi and has profound significance for the composition of the veda. The gods are called upon to inspire the apprehension of this insight (often termed "causing it to flow"), leading to the composition of a mantra (e.g., the famous sAvitrI mantra of vishvAmitra gAthina). 

This concept was central to the early Indo-Iranian religion because it appears on the Iranic side as daenA, which eventually came to mean religion. It is notable that, on the Iranic side, daenA is personified as a female deity, sometimes identified with the goddess ashi vanghuhi, or mentioned as her sister. We posit that such Indo-Iranian border-zone developments led to the emergence of a comparable goddess gAyatrI as the personification of the dhI mentioned in the sAvitrI. As we have noted before, on the Hindu side, a similar personification is also seen in the goddess AkUtI.
